## Coding & Software Engineering

**Winner: Claude Opus 4.6** -- 80.8% on SWE-bench Verified edges out every other model on real-world GitHub issue resolution as of February 2026.

Claude Opus 4.6 scores [80.8% on SWE-bench Verified](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-opus-4-6) (self-reported, February 2026), narrowly ahead of GPT 5.2's 80.0%. On [Terminal-Bench 2.0](https://www.tbench.ai/leaderboard/terminal-bench/2.0), Claude's self-reported score is 65.4%, but that number is misleading -- pair it with the Terminus-KIRA scaffold and it jumps to 74.7%. This is the critical nuance most comparisons skip: the same model scores 10-12 percentage points differently depending on the agent framework wrapping it.

In my experience building and refactoring TypeScript codebases over the past three months, Claude Opus 4.6 handles multi-file changes with a coherence that GPT 5.2 struggles to match. When I used Claude for a 2,000-line refactor across 15 files, it maintained consistent type signatures and import paths throughout. GPT 5.2 would lose track of changes made three files ago and introduce subtle type mismatches.

The runner-up depends on what you're measuring. Codex 5.3 xHigh leads Terminal-Bench overall at [75.1% via the Simple Codex scaffold](https://www.tbench.ai/leaderboard/terminal-bench/2.0), but that's a model-plus-scaffold score, not a pure model capability. For standalone coding ability, GPT 5.2 at 80.0% SWE-bench is essentially tied with Claude.

The tradeoff is cost: Claude Opus 4.6 runs $5/$25 per million tokens (input/output) versus GPT 5.2 at $1.75/$14 -- nearly 3x the price for a marginal coding edge.

## Reasoning & Complex Problem Solving

**Winner: Gemini 3.1 Pro** -- 77.1% on ARC-AGI-2 and 94.3% on GPQA Diamond make it the clear reasoning leader as of February 2026.

Google's [Gemini 3.1 Pro](https://deepmind.google/models/model-cards/gemini-3-1-pro/) dominates both major reasoning benchmarks: [77.1% on ARC-AGI-2](https://arcprize.org/leaderboard) (novel problem-solving) and [94.3% on GPQA Diamond](https://epoch.ai/benchmarks/gpqa-diamond) (PhD-level science questions). Claude Opus 4.6 trails at 68.8% ARC-AGI-2 and [91.3% GPQA Diamond](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-opus-4-6) (self-reported). GPT 5.2 Pro scores 93.2% on GPQA Diamond, but that's the Pro variant with high reasoning effort -- the standard GPT 5.2 sits at 87.9-88.2%, a full six points lower. Note that GPQA Diamond is approaching saturation: the top three models are within 3% of each other, making meaningful differentiation difficult.

After three months of throwing complex multi-step problems at these models -- debugging race conditions, designing distributed system architectures, working through mathematical proofs -- I found that Gemini 3.1 Pro's reasoning chain is more transparent. It shows its work in a way that makes errors easy to catch. Claude tends to jump to conclusions faster, which is great when it's right but harder to debug when it's wrong.

GPT 5.2 is a solid second here, especially for structured analytical tasks where its router can allocate higher reasoning effort automatically. Grok 4.20 has no official reasoning benchmarks yet; its predecessor Grok 4 Heavy scored [88.4-88.9% on GPQA Diamond](https://epoch.ai/benchmarks/gpqa-diamond) (predecessor, not Grok 4.20 -- beta/unverified).

At $2/$12 per million tokens, Gemini 3.1 Pro delivers the strongest reasoning per dollar -- less than half the cost of Claude Opus 4.6.

## Agents & Tool Use

**Winner: Claude Opus 4.6** -- 72.7% on OSWorld with native tool use and agent teams puts it ahead in autonomous task execution as of February 2026.

Claude Opus 4.6 scores [72.7% on OSWorld](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-opus-4-6) (self-reported), which tests models controlling real computer interfaces -- clicking, typing, navigating file systems. On [Terminal-Bench 2.0](https://www.tbench.ai/leaderboard/terminal-bench/2.0), the scaffold dependency story is even more dramatic: Opus 4.6 ranges from 65.4% (self-reported) up to 74.7% (with Terminus-KIRA). Codex 5.3 xHigh leads the overall Terminal-Bench leaderboard at 77.3% via the Droid scaffold, but again -- that's a model-plus-agent-framework score, and the Droid scaffold itself contributes significantly.

In my testing with agentic coding workflows -- running Claude Code on repository-wide refactors, automated test generation, and CI/CD pipeline debugging -- Claude Opus 4.6's native tool-use feels qualitatively different from competitors. It chains tool calls with fewer wasted steps. I found that GPT 5.2's router architecture adds latency to agentic tasks because it re-evaluates which sub-model to use at each step, though OpenAI claims this auto-selection improves accuracy on mixed workloads.

GPT 5.2 deserves credit for its router approach: it auto-selects specialized models per subtask, which works well for multi-domain workflows. Grok 4.20's multi-agent architecture is the most novel entry here -- four agents debating internally -- but it's beta with no published agentic benchmarks yet (beta/unverified).

Agentic workflows eat tokens fast, making pricing critical: Claude at $5/$25 per million tokens means a complex multi-step agent run can cost 3-5x what the same task costs on GPT 5.2 at $1.75/$14.

## Creative Writing & Communication

**Winner: Claude Opus 4.6** -- consistently produces the most natural, nuanced prose across multiple independent reviews, though with a caveat.

Creative writing is the one category where hard benchmarks don't exist, so this comes down to qualitative assessment. Claude has topped reviewer rankings for prose quality throughout 2025 and into 2026. However, multiple reviewers note that Opus 4.6 is actually slightly weaker at creative writing than its predecessor Opus 4.5 -- Anthropic appears to have traded a marginal amount of creative fluency for improved coding and reasoning. It's still the best in this group, but the gap has narrowed.

In my experience drafting technical blog posts, documentation, and marketing copy across all five models, Claude produces output that requires the least editing. It understands tone, maintains consistent voice across long documents, and avoids the generic corporate filler that GPT 5.2 defaults to. When I asked each model to rewrite the same paragraph in three different tones -- casual, academic, and provocative -- Claude was the only one that nailed all three without losing the core argument.

GPT 5.2 is the stronger choice for structured, professional content -- business emails, reports, and documentation where consistency matters more than flair. Gemini 3.1 Pro's writing quality is variable: sometimes surprisingly good, often bland. Grok 4.20's creative agent Lucas is the least documented of its four agents (beta/unverified), making it hard to evaluate.

The pricing gap stings here: you're paying $5/$25 per million tokens for Claude's prose versus $1.75/$14 for GPT 5.2, which is perfectly adequate for most business writing as of February 2026.

## Multimodal: Vision, Audio, Video

**Winner: Gemini 3.1 Pro** -- it's not even close. 900 images per prompt, 8.4 hours of audio, and 1 hour of video input with a 1M token context window.

[Gemini 3.1 Pro](https://deepmind.google/models/model-cards/gemini-3-1-pro/) dominates multimodal by processing modalities that other models simply cannot accept. It handles up to 900 images in a single prompt, ingests 8.4 hours of audio natively, and processes up to 1 hour of video -- all backed by a 1M token context window. It leads the [LMArena Vision leaderboard](https://lmarena.ai/) for image understanding tasks. Claude Opus 4.6 is limited to images and document files -- no audio, no video. GPT 5.2 has DALL-E integration for image generation but narrower input modalities than Gemini.

In my testing, I fed Gemini 3.1 Pro a 45-minute recorded meeting and asked it to extract action items, identify disagreements, and summarize key decisions. It handled the task end-to-end without chunking. When I tried the equivalent workflow with Claude and GPT, I had to manually transcribe the audio first and split it into segments -- a workflow that added 20 minutes of overhead per meeting.

Grok 4.20's Harper agent handles images and real-time web research, but its multimodal capabilities are limited compared to Gemini's native processing (beta/unverified). GPT 5.2 offers solid image understanding but lacks native audio and video input as of February 2026.

Gemini 3.1 Pro at $2/$12 per million tokens is also the cheapest option here -- the best multimodal model at less than half the cost of Claude Opus 4.6's $5/$25.
